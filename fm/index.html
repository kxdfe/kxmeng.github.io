Trigonometric functions

  1. Understand and use the definitions of sine, cosine and tangent for all arguments; their graphs, symmetries and periodicity
  2. Know and use exact values of \(\sin\), \(\cos\) and \(\tan\) for \(0, \dfrac{\pi}{6}, \dfrac{\pi}{4}, \dfrac{\pi}{3}, \dfrac{\pi}{2}, \pi\) and multiples thereof
  3. Understand and use \(\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\) and \(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\)
  4. Solve simple trigonometric equations in a given interval, including quadratic equations in \(\sin, \cos\) and \(\tan\) and equations involving multiples of the unknown angle
  5. Construct proofs involving trigonometric functions and identities

Iteration

  1. Locate roots of \(f(x) = 0\) by considering changes of sign of \(f(x)\) in an interval of \(x\) on which \(f(x)\) is sufficiently well behaved (continuous)
  2. Understand how change of sign methods can fail e.g. when the interval is too large sign may not change as there may be an even number of roots, or if the function is not continuous
  3. Solve equations approximately using simple iterative methods; be able to draw associated cobweb and staircase diagrams

Complex numbers

  1. Know the meaning of "real part", "imaginary part", "conjugate", "modulus" and "argument" of a complex number
  2. Be able to express a complex number \(z\) in either cartesian form or modulus-argument form, and convert between the two forms
  3. Understand and be able to use the notation: \(z, z^*, \mathrm{Re}(z), \mathrm{Im}(z), \mathrm{arg}(z), |z|\)
  4. Uderstand and be able to use the exponential form of a complex number
  5. Be able to carry out basic arithmetic operations on complex numbers in both cartesian and modulus-argument forms
  6. Be able to find algebraically the two square roots of a complex number
  7. Be able to use and interpret Argand diagrams
  8. Understand the geometrical effects of taking the conjugate of a complex number, and adding and subtracting two complex numbers
  9. Understand the geometrical effects of multiplying and dividing two complex numbers
  10. Know and be able to use Euler’s formula \(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\)
  11. Be able to illustrate equations and inequalities involving complex numbers by means of loci in an Argand diagram
  12. Understand and be able to use set notation in the context of loci

Vectors

  1. Understand and be able to use the equation of a straight line and a plane, in 2D and 3D, in cartesian and vector form
  2. Be able to calculate the scalar product and use it both to calculate the angles between vectors and/or lines, and also as a test for perpendicularity
  3. Be able to find, where it exists, the point of intersection between two lines
  4. Be able to use the vector product to find a vector perpendicular to two given vectors
  5. Be able to find the distance between two parallel lines and the shortest distance between two skew lines
  6. Be able to find the shortest distance between a point and a line

Matrices

  1. Understand the meaning of "conformable", "equal", "square", "rectangular", "m by n", "determinant", "zero" and "null", "transpose" and "identity" when applied to matrices
  2. Be able to add, subtract and multiply conformable matrices; multiply a matrix by a scalar
  3. Understand that matrix multiplication is associative but not commutative
  4. Be able to find and use matrices to represent linear transformations in 2D: reflections in either axis or the line \(y=\pm x\), rotation about the origin, enlargement centred at the origin, stretch or shear parallel to either axis
  5. Be able to find and use matrices to represent successive transformations, including understanding that the matrix product AB is the transformation B followed by A
  6. Be able to use matrices to represent single linear transformations in 3D
  7. Be able to find invariant points and lines for a linear transformation
